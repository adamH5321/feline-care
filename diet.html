Cat Diets by Age

Cats have different dietary needs at various stages of their lives:

  • Kittens (0-6 months): Require a diet rich in protein and calories for growth. Typically, feed them high-quality kitten-specific wet or dry cat food formulated to meet their nutritional needs. A general guideline is to provide 3-4 meals per day, with approximately 1/4 to 1/3 cup of dry cat food per meal, supplemented with one to two 3-ounce cans of wet cat food per day.
  • Adult Cats (7 months - 6 years): Require a well-balanced diet, which typically includes a combination of high-quality wet and dry cat food. A general guideline is to feed an adult cat around 1/2 to 1 cup of dry cat food per day, supplemented with one to two 3-ounce cans of wet cat food.
  • Mature Cats (7-10 years): Require a balanced diet that supports their maintenance needs, with slightly reduced calorie intake compared to their adult years. Typically, provide around 1/3 to 1/2 cup of high-quality dry cat food per day, along with one to two 3-ounce cans of wet cat food.
  • Senior Cats (11-14 years): Benefit from a diet tailored to their age, often including specially formulated senior cat food with added nutrients to support joint and dental health. Typically, provide around 1/4 to 1/3 cup of high-quality dry cat food per day, supplemented with one to two 3-ounce cans of wet cat food. These added nutrients can help maintain their overall health and comfort in their senior years.
  • Super Senior Cats (15+ years): Require a specialized diet formulated to meet their specific needs in their advanced years. Choose senior cat food with added nutrients to support joint, dental, and overall health. Typically, provide around 1/4 to 1/3 cup of high-quality dry cat food per day, supplemented with one to two 3-ounce cans of wet cat food. Adjust the portions based on your cat's specific age, weight, and activity level. These added nutrients can contribute to their comfort and well-being in their golden years. Regular vet check-ups become even more important to address age-related health concerns.

Human Foods Safe for Cats

While cats primarily need cat food, some human foods are safe for occasional treats:

  • Cooked Chicken or Turkey: Plain, boneless, and skinless meat is a favorite among many cats.
  • Cooked Fish: Salmon and tuna, without added seasonings or spices, are occasional treats.
  • Carrots: Cooked or steamed carrots can be a healthy snack, but cats are obligate carnivores and don't require vegetables in their diet.
  • Cooked Eggs: Plain scrambled or boiled eggs can provide a protein boost.
  • Plain Rice: Plain, cooked white or brown rice may be offered in small amounts.
  • Cooked Oatmeal: Plain oatmeal, cooked with water and no added sugar, can be a source of fiber.
  • Pumpkin: Canned, unsweetened pumpkin (not pumpkin pie filling) can aid digestion.
  • Cantaloupe: Small amounts of fresh cantaloupe are safe as an occasional treat.
  • Blueberries: A few fresh, washed blueberries can serve as a healthy treat.

Foods Toxic to Cats

Never feed these foods to your cat, as they can be toxic and harmful:

  • Chocolate: Contains theobromine and caffeine, which are toxic to cats and can cause vomiting, diarrhea, seizures, and even death.
  • Grapes and Raisins: Can lead to kidney failure in cats, with even small amounts causing severe issues.
  • Onions and Garlic: Contain compounds that can damage a cat's red blood cells, leading to anemia
  • Alcohol: Even small amounts of alcohol can be extremely toxic and potentially fatal to cats.
  • Caffeine: Found in coffee, tea, and some energy drinks, caffeine can cause rapid heart rate, restlessness, and tremors in cats.
  • Bones: Cooked bones can splinter and cause choking or internal injuries in cats.
  • Xylitol: A sugar substitute found in many sugar-free products like gum and candy, xylitol can lead to insulin release, causing hypoglycemia and liver damage.
  • Raw Meat and Fish: May contain harmful bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli, and can lead to nutritional imbalances over time.
  • Dairy Products: While many cats are lactose intolerant and can develop digestive issues from milk and dairy products.
  • Avocado: Contains a substance called persin, which is toxic to cats and can cause gastrointestinal problems.
  • Bones and Fat Trimmings: Can lead to digestive obstructions and pancreatitis, respectively.
